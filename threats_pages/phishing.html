Phishing

Description: Fraudulent attempts to obtain sensitive information (like login credentials) by posing as a trustworthy entity, typically through email or messaging.

Impact: Data theft, unauthorized access, financial fraud.

Examples: Spear-phishing (targeted attacks), Business Email Compromise (BEC).

Phishing attacks are among the most common cyber threats, targeting individuals and organizations through deceptive emails, messages, or websites designed to steal sensitive information.

Here are some best practices for protecting against phishing threats:

  1. Employee Education and Awareness
    • Regular training on how to identify phishing attempts is crucial. Ensure employees know how to spot suspicious emails, links, and attachments.
    • Conduct phishing simulations to test employees' ability to recognize phishing attempts.
  2. Email Filtering and Spam Protection
    • Use advanced email filtering solutions to block phishing emails before they reach users’ inboxes.
    • Implement anti-phishing tools that analyze incoming emails for suspicious content, such as phishing links or spoofed sender addresses.
    • Mark external emails with a warning banner, so users know if an email originates outside the organization.
  3. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
    • Implement multi-factor authentication (MFA) across all accounts, especially for sensitive or privileged accounts.
    • Even if a phishing attack is successful and credentials are compromised, MFA provides an extra layer of security, making it harder for attackers to gain access.
  4. Use Strong, Unique Passwords
    • Ensure all users create strong, unique passwords for each account. Avoid reusing passwords across different services, which can prevent attackers from accessing multiple accounts with stolen credentials.
    • Encourage the use of password managers to store and generate strong passwords.
  5. Enable Web Filtering
    • Implement web filtering to block access to known malicious websites or phishing sites.
    • Use tools like Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting & Conformance (DMARC), Sender Policy Framework (SPF), and DomainKeys Identified Mail (DKIM) to verify email sender domains and prevent email spoofing.
  6. Verify Emails and Links Before Clicking
    • Always check the sender's email address carefully, as phishing emails often use addresses that look similar to legitimate ones (e.g., "support@paypaI.com" instead of "support@paypal.com").
    • Hover over links in emails to verify the URL before clicking. Avoid clicking on links that don’t match the organization’s domain.
    • Encourage the use of official websites by typing URLs directly into the browser, rather than clicking on links from emails.
  7. Avoid Downloading Attachments from Untrusted Sources
    • Do not open attachments from unknown or suspicious emails. Phishing emails often contain malicious attachments like PDFs, Word documents, or Excel files that can infect your system with malware or ransomware.
    • Consider disabling macros in Microsoft Office files, which are often used by attackers to execute malicious code.
  8. Implement Email Authentication Standards (DMARC, SPF, DKIM)
    • Deploy DMARC, SPF, and DKIM standards for email authentication to prevent attackers from spoofing your organization's domain.
    • These tools help verify that emails claiming to be from your domain are legitimate, reducing the risk of phishing attacks that use forged sender addresses.

If You Suspect a Phishing Attack:

  • Do not click links or download attachments: If the email looks suspicious, avoid interacting with any content.
  • Verify the request: If it appears to come from a trusted source (e.g., a bank or company), contact them directly through official channels to confirm.
  • Report the phishing email: Alert your IT or security team and submit the email for review.
  • Change passwords immediately: If you’ve inadvertently provided credentials, change your passwords as soon as possible and enable multi-factor authentication.
